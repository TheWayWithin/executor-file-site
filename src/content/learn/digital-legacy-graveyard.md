End-of-life planning services make an unusual promise: that they will still be there, working, on a day that might be thirty years away, for a grieving stranger who has never used them. It is worth looking at how that promise has held up.

**Lantern** offered end-of-life planning checklists and document storage. When it retired the service, users were given a support contact and a deadline to extract their own plans. The people who had done the responsible thing, in the responsible place, got homework and a countdown.

**Deathswitch** was an early dead man's switch: it emailed your secrets to chosen people if you stopped responding to prompts. The service that existed to outlive its users did not outlive its decade.

**Legacy Locker** was one of the first digital-estate vaults, storing credentials and last wishes. It was acquired in 2013 and the product no longer exists.

**Beyond** was a UK funeral and estate comparison service. Its domain now serves another company's content, which is its own kind of epitaph.

**Farewill** is the gentler case: the UK online will-writer was sold to Dignity in 2024. Customers' documents moved with the business. An acquisition is an ownership change, not a shutdown, and it is fair to say so. It is also a reminder that the company holding your family's plans can become a different company without asking you.

The American vaults **Everplans** and **Cake** have both been acquired too, and both now sit inside the funeral-services sector. Again: ownership changes, stated as such. The pattern is not that these companies were bad. Most were thoughtful products run by people who cared. The pattern is structural: a hosted service is a bet that a company outlives you, keeps its terms, and keeps its door open, and in this category that bet keeps losing.

## What to do instead

The uncomfortable conclusion is not "avoid planning tools". It is: keep the record your family will need in a form that has no company in it.

- Paper survives every acquisition. A printed letter of instruction with the will beats a vault nobody can open.
- Files survive too, if they are in open formats. A plain text register outlives any app that could have displayed it.
- If it is sensitive, encrypt it with standard, open tools rather than a service's proprietary lock.
